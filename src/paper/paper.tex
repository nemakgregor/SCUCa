\begin{abstract}
Security-Constrained Unit Commitment is a large-scale optimization problem for scheduling day-ahead power generation. The objective is to determine the least-cost commitment and dispatch of generators while satisfying network limits and the N-1 security criterion. These constraints ensure system stability following any single line failure. Security constraints make the underlying mixed-integer linear program computationally inefficient for large systems.

This paper presents a hybrid framework to accelerate the optimization process by integrating machine learning techniques with a traditional solver. Have been tested k-nearest neighbor warm-starts to provide initial solutions, branching hints to guide the solver’s search process, and methods for redundant constraint identification to reduce problem size.

Computational experiments demonstrate that the integrated approach achieves speedups of up to 10 times over a standard solver baseline. This acceleration is achieved without compromising the feasibility or optimality of the final solution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
This paper is devoted to the Security-Constrained Unit Commitment (SCUC) problem, a key optimization task in the operation of modern power systems. The objective is to determine a least-cost schedule of generator commitment and dispatch for a set time horizon, while respecting all physical and operational limits. The SCUC is a core component of day-ahead electricity markets, and its solution must be obtained reliably under time limits.

The primary computational difficulty of this problem, which is formulated as a mixed-integer linear program (MILP), arises from two main sources. The first involves the complex inter-temporal dynamics of thermal generators. These include binary on/off decisions that are coupled across time by constraints such as minimum up and down times, startup cost profiles, and physical ramping limitations. These characteristics prevent the problem from being easily decomposed and solved for each time period independently.

The second and often more significant challenge is the enforcement of N-1 security constraints. This requirement forces that the power system must remain in a stable operating state following the unexpected failure of any single component, such as a transmission line or a generator. For each time period, this introduces a large number of contingency scenarios that must be explicitly modeled and checked. In typical formulations, the number of these security constraints can scale quadratically with the number of transmission lines, quickly making the problem computationally ineffective for realistic grid models.

Due to this large scale and complexity, applying a standard MILP solver directly is often not a viable approach for practical applications. The integration of variable renewable energy sources further exacerbates this issue by introducing greater uncertainty and operational stress. Even with state-of-the-art solvers, large-scale SCUC instances frequently remain intractable. It means a feasible and optimal solution cannot be found within the strict time limits required for day-ahead market clearing. This difficulty pushes to create advanced methods that make solving faster while still keeping the solution optimality and stable.

\section{Literature review}
The formulations for the SCUC problem have evolved from early heuristics and Lagrangian relaxation~\cite{Merlin1983} to modern MILP models. A computationally efficient MILP formulation for thermal unit commitment with piecewise-linear costs was introduced in~\cite{Carrion2006}, which has become a foundational model. More accurate formulations for complex unit dynamics, such as minimum up/down logic and ramping, have since been developed~\cite{Ostrowski2012}. For handling larger systems, decomposition methods such as Benders' decomposition and Lagrangian relaxation are often used to separate the main commitment problem, which involves integer variables, from the network and security subproblems that involve continuous variables~\cite{Morales2013, Pan2016}.

On the network modeling side, the DC power flow approximation is the standard for SCUC due to its linearity and scalability. Power Transfer Distribution Factors (PTDFs) are widely used to model base-case flows, while Line Outage Distribution Factors (LODFs) provide an efficient way to approximate post-contingency flows without solving the power flow problem for each contingency~\cite{Christie2000, Tejada2018}. Although more accurate AC models have been proposed, their non-convexity makes them computationally intractable for most practical unit commitment problems~\cite{Knueven2018}. To manage the quadratic growth of contingency constraints, a dynamic constraint generation technique is often adopted. In this approach, only a subset of potentially binding constraints is included initially, and additional constraints are added as needed during the branch-and-cut process~\cite{Tejada2018}. A complementary approach involves screening techniques that aim to discard provably redundant contingencies a priori using network sensitivities or historical data~\cite{Holzer2024}.

More recently, machine learning (ML) has emerged as a powerful tool for accelerating the solution of MILPs~\cite{Bengio2021}. In power systems operations, ML has been applied to various problems, including directly predicting unit commitment schedules~\cite{Xavier2019, Quarm2021}, screening active constraints in optimal power flow problems~\cite{Donti2021}, and learning high-quality warm starts for MILP models~\cite{JimenezCordero2022}. While these methods offer benefits such as finding high-quality solutions more quickly, safety is a primary concern, as an incorrect warm start or constraint screening can compromise system reliability. This concern is addressed in this paper by presenting a framework that integrates machine learning accelerators with an exact MILP model. In this work, various combinations of the proposed approaches are investigated to identify an optimal configuration.

\section{Model Definition}

A deterministic SCUC problem is solved over a discrete set of time periods $\mathcal{T}=\{0,\dots,T-1\}$. The power system comprises a set of thermal generating units $\mathcal{G}$, buses $\mathcal{B}$, transmission lines $\mathcal{L}$, and reserve products $\mathcal{R}$. The electrical network is modeled using the DC approximation. In this model, base-case flows are calculated using Power Transfer Distribution Factors (PTDFs), and the impacts of line outages are modeled with Line Outage Distribution Factors (LODFs).

The primary decision variables are defined for each thermal generator $g\in\mathcal{G}$ and time period $t\in\mathcal{T}$ as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Commitment status $u_{g,t}\in\{0,1\}$, which indicates if the generator is on or off.
    \item Startup and shutdown indicators $v_{g,t}, w_{g,t}\in\{0,1\}$, which indicate a change in commitment status.
    \item Segmented power output $p^{\mathrm{seg}}_{g,t,s}\ge 0$, representing the power produced in each segment $s$ of the generator's piecewise-linear cost curve.
    \item Total generation $p_{g,t}$, defined as $p_{g,t}=u_{g,t}\underline{P}_{g,t}+\sum_{s}p^{\mathrm{seg}}_{g,t,s}$, where $\underline{P}_{g,t}$ is the minimum stable generation level.
    \item Reserve provision $r_{k,g,t}\ge 0$ for each reserve product $k\in\mathcal{R}$.
    \item System-wide reserve shortfall $s_{k,t}\ge 0$ for each reserve product $k\in\mathcal{R}$.
\end{itemize}

For the transmission network, the variables for each line $\ell\in\mathcal{L}$ and time $t\in\mathcal{T}$ are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Base-case power flow $f_{\ell,t}\in\mathbb{R}$.
    \item Base-case overflow slack variables $\overline{o}_{\ell,t},\underline{o}_{\ell,t}\ge 0$.
    \item Shared N-1 contingency overflow slack variables $\overline{c}_{\ell,t},\underline{c}_{\ell,t}\ge 0$.
\end{itemize}

The operational logic for thermal units is enforced through a set of inter-temporal constraints. The power produced in each segment is constrained by the unit's commitment status and physical capacity, $A_{g,s,t}$:
\begin{equation}
0\le p^{\mathrm{seg}}_{g,t,s} \le A_{g,s,t}\,u_{g,t}, \ \forall g,t,s. \label{eq:seg-link}
\end{equation}
The system-wide power balance must be maintained at each time period, with total generation equal to total demand, $D_{b,t}$:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{g}\Big(u_{g,t}\underline{P}_{g,t}+\sum_{s}p^{\mathrm{seg}}_{g,t,s}\Big)=\sum_{b}D_{b,t}, \ \forall t. \label{eq:balance}
\end{equation}
The availability of reserves is limited by the capacity between the current operating point of a unit and its maximum output, $\overline{P}_{g,t}$:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{s}p^{\mathrm{seg}}_{g,t,s}+\sum_{k}r_{k,g,t}\le (\overline{P}_{g,t}-\underline{P}_{g,t})u_{g,t}, \ \forall g,t. \label{eq:res-head}
\end{equation}
The total reserve provided must be equal to the system requirement $R_{k,t}$ minus penalized shortfall:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{g}r_{k,g,t} + s_{k,t} \ge R_{k,t}, \ \forall k,t. \label{eq:res-req}
\end{equation}
Logical constraints define the startup and shutdown events based on changes in commitment status:
\begin{align}
u_{g,t}-u_{g,t-1} &= v_{g,t}-w_{g,t}, \label{eq:su-def}\\
v_{g,t}+w_{g,t} &\le 1. \label{eq:su-excl}
\end{align}
Ramping constraints limit the rate of change of power output between periods based on ramp-up ($RU_g$), ramp-down ($RD_g$), startup ($SU_g$), and shutdown ($SD_g$) limits:
\begin{align}
p_{g,t}-p_{g,t-1} &\le RU_g\,u_{g,t-1} + SU_g\,v_{g,t}, \label{eq:ramp-up}\\
p_{g,t-1}-p_{g,t} &\le RD_g\,u_{g,t} + SD_g\,w_{g,t}. \label{eq:ramp-dn}
\end{align}
Minimum up-time ($U_g$) and down-time ($D_g$) requirements are enforced:
\begin{align}
\sum_{\tau=t-U_g+1}^{t} v_{g,\tau} \le u_{g,t}, \quad
\sum_{\tau=t-D_g+1}^{t} w_{g,\tau} \le 1-u_{g,t}. \label{eq:min-ud}
\end{align}

The DC power flow model provides a linear approximation of grid physics. The net power injection at each bus is defined:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{inj}_{b,t}=\sum_{g\in\mathcal{G}_b} p_{g,t} - D_{b,t}.
\end{equation}
The base-case flow on each line $\ell$ is then calculated as a linear combination of these injections, weighted by the pre-computed PTDFs:
\begin{equation}
f_{\ell,t}=\sum_{b\neq b^{\mathrm{ref}}}\mathrm{PTDF}_{\ell,b}\,\mathrm{inj}_{b,t}. \label{eq:flow-def}
\end{equation}
These flows must respect the line's normal thermal rating, $F^{\mathrm{N}}_{\ell,t}$, with any violation captured by slack variables:
\begin{align}
f_{\ell,t} \le F^{\mathrm{N}}_{\ell,t}+\overline{o}_{\ell,t}, \quad
-f_{\ell,t} \le F^{\mathrm{N}}_{\ell,t}+\underline{o}_{\ell,t}. \label{eq:base-lims}
\end{align}
To ensure N-1 security, the post-contingency flow on a monitored line $\ell$ following the outage of a line $m$ is approximated using the LODF:
\begin{equation}
f_{\ell,t}^{(m)} \approx f_{\ell,t}+\mathrm{LODF}_{\ell,m}f_{m,t}. \label{eq:lodf}
\end{equation}
These post-contingency flows must remain within a higher emergency rating, $F^{\mathrm{E}}_{\ell,t}$. A single pair of shared slack variables is used per monitored line to capture the worst-case violation across all contingencies:
\begin{align}
f_{\ell,t}+\mathrm{LODF}_{\ell,m}f_{m,t} &\le F^{\mathrm{E}}_{\ell,t}+\overline{c}_{\ell,t}, \label{eq:cont-line-pos}\\
-f_{\ell,t}-\mathrm{LODF}_{\ell,m}f_{m,t} &\le F^{\mathrm{E}}_{\ell,t}+\underline{c}_{\ell,t}. \label{eq:cont-line-neg}
\end{align}


The objective function minimizes the total system operating cost, which includes production costs, startup costs, and penalties for constraint violations.
\begin{align}
\min \ \ & \sum_{t}\sum_{g}\Big( C^{\min}_{g,t}\,u_{g,t} + \sum_{s\in\mathcal{K}_g} C^{\mathrm{seg}}_{g,s,t}\, p^{\mathrm{seg}}_{g,t,s} \Big) \nonumber\\
& + \sum_{t}\sum_{g} C^{\mathrm{su}}_g\, v_{g,t} \label{eq:obj}\\
& + \sum_{t}\sum_{k\in\mathcal{R}} \pi^{\mathrm{res}}_k\, s_{k,t} \nonumber\\
& + \sum_{t}\sum_{\ell\in\mathcal{L}} \pi^{\mathrm{flow}}_{\ell,t}(\overline{o}_{\ell,t}+\underline{o}_{\ell,t}) \nonumber\\
& + \gamma \sum_{t}\sum_{\ell\in\mathcal{L}} \pi^{\mathrm{flow}}_{\ell,t}(\overline{c}_{\ell,t}+\underline{c}_{\ell,t}). \nonumber
\end{align}
The terms in \eqref{eq:obj} are defined as follows. The first line represents production costs, where $C^{\min}_{g,t}$ is the no-load cost and $C^{\mathrm{seg}}_{g,s,t}$ is the marginal cost for energy in each segment. The second term accounts for startup costs, $C^{\mathrm{su}}_g$. The remaining terms are high-cost penalties for violating soft constraints: $\pi^{\mathrm{res}}_k$ penalizes shortfalls in reserve requirements, while $\pi^{\mathrm{flow}}_{\ell,t}$ penalizes base-case and post-contingency transmission overflows. The multiplier $\gamma>1$ is used to prioritize resolving contingency violations.


\section{Acceleration Framework}
Several acceleration mechanisms are investigated, each identified by a short label for reference in the computational experiments.

\paragraph{Constraint Screening (PRUNE-$\tau$, GNN).}
Given that the number of post-contingency transmission constraints can be prohibitively large, screening methods are applied to reduce the set of constraints considered by the solver. A lightweight screening method is based on a 1-nearest-neighbor (1-NN) algorithm. Each problem instance is represented by a z-scored load vector. For a new instance, the solution from the nearest neighbor in a database of previously solved cases is used to estimate post-contingency flows using the target scenario's Line Outage Distribution Factor (LODF) and Power Transfer Distribution Factor (PTDF) matrices. For each monitored line $\ell$ and contingency $m$, the minimum relative emergency margin across time is computed:
\begin{equation}
s^{m}_{\ell}=\min_t \frac{F^{\mathrm{E}}_{\ell,t}-|f_{\ell,t}+\mathrm{LODF}_{\ell,m}f_{m,t}|}{F^{\mathrm{E}}_{\ell,t}}.
\end{equation}
Contingency pairs with $s_{\ell}^m \ge\tau$ are pruned for all time periods. This technique is labeled as PRUNE-$\tau$, where a larger $\tau$ indicates more aggressive pruning.

An alternative screening approach utilizes a Graph Neural Network (GNN) to score the criticality of each transmission line. The network operates on a line graph where nodes represent transmission lines and edges connect lines that share a bus. A two-layer GraphSAGE model is trained using Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss to produce a per-line criticality score. At inference, a contingency is kept only if one of its associated lines is predicted to be critical. This optional screening layer is labeled as GNN.

\paragraph{Lazy Enforcement (LAZY, BANDIT).}
Instead of adding all post-contingency limits a priori, N-1 security is enforced using a lazy constraint callback. At each incumbent solution found during the branch-and-cut process, post-contingency flows are calculated. If any flow violates its emergency limit, a linear inequality is generated and added to the model. This approach keeps the root Linear Program (LP) compact while guaranteeing the final solution is N-1 secure. This base technique is labeled as LAZY.

An optional modification adds only the top-$K$ most violated contingencies at each incumbent.  A more adaptive approach uses a multi-armed bandit to dynamically select the parameter $K$. Each ``arm'' of the bandit corresponds to a candidate value. After a new incumbent solution is found, a reward is computed based on metrics such as gap improvement per unit of time. The value of $K$ for the next incumbent is then selected based on an $\varepsilon$-greedy rule that balances exploration and exploitation. This adaptive variant is labeled as BANDIT.

\paragraph{Warm Starts (WARM, GRU).}
To accelerate the discovery of the first feasible solution, the MILP solver is warm-started using initial values derived from ML models. One approach is a memory-light 1-NN method that matches an instance to its nearest neighbor in a historical database based on system load features. The commitment schedules, segment powers, and reserve provisions from the historical solution are then used to populate the start attributes for the variables in the new problem. This technique is labeled as WARM.

A more granular warm start for generator dispatch profiles is generated using a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) network. A two-layer GRU is trained with Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss to predict the fraction of available headroom used by each generator at each hour. This provides a coherent dispatch trajectory that can be used to initialize the power segment variables. This optional dispatch warm start is labeled as GRU.

\paragraph{Branching Guidance (HINTS, COMMIT).}
In addition to providing a warm start, ML models are used to guide the solver's branching decisions. The commitment values from a warm-start solution can be directly translated into branching hints for the solver by setting the variable hint value attribute and assigning a time-biased branching priority to encourage exploration of early time periods first. This technique is labeled as HINTS.

A more advanced method uses a dedicated classifier, such as a Gradient Boosted Decision Tree (GBDT), which is trained on historical data to predict the commitment probability for each generator at each time period. These probabilities are then translated into hints and priorities to influence the solver's search order. This technique is labeled as COMMIT.

\section{Conclusion}

This study demonstrates that the most effective way to accelerate a raw mixed-integer linear programming (MILP) formulation of the security-constrained unit commitment (SCUC) problem is to reduce or delay post-contingency constraints while preserving exact feasibility checks. Across the benchmark suite considered here, two strategies consistently provide the largest gains: screening redundant contingencies before the solve using data-driven predictors and PTDF/LODF sensitivities, and generating only the necessary contingency constraints on demand during branch-and-cut.

Conservative screening thresholds and lazy generation preserve robustness while substantially reducing end-to-end runtime on the larger systems. By contrast, learning signals applied only to the discrete search, such as warm starts and branching hints, provide smaller average gains but still help stabilize the search and improve the quality of early incumbents. These mechanisms can be combined effectively, suggesting a constraint-economy-first design: shrink or postpone the contingency set, verify security exactly, and then add inexpensive learning cues to guide the solver.

Future work will focus on adapting screening thresholds online, coupling screening with lazy generation in a unified policy, and developing reliability-aware predictors with stronger guarantees.
