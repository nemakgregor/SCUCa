Thank you for the careful and useful reviews. We revised the paper to make the scope more modest and clearer. The paper is now presented as a comparison of acceleration approaches for DC-SCUC, not as a new SCUC formulation or a new ML solver. We also clarified the feasibility audit, shortened the manuscript, removed the duplicate figure, and kept the new evidence in the main text.

\section*{Response to Reviewer 1}

\textbf{Comment 1. Constraint reduction methodology is unclear; show initial and reduced constraint numbers.}

We expanded the PRUNE and LAZY descriptions. The paper now explains that PRUNE removes constraints before optimization and must be checked by a full post-solve N-1 audit, while LAZY adds violated contingency constraints during callbacks. We also added a compact constraint-reduction table in the main Results section. It reports rows relative to RAW, explicit N-1 constraints removed, and lazy cuts added.

\textbf{Comment 2. The abstract claim of 10x speedup is questionable.}

We changed the abstract. It now states the measured values more precisely: up to 25.8x on case89pegase and 9.6x on average across the six benchmark cases. We no longer describe the result as a generic 10x speedup over baseline solvers.

\textbf{Comment 3. Prior ML-SCUC speedups are not discussed.}

We added a comparison in the Introduction. The revised text discusses prior ML-based SCUC and warm-starting results, explains how their reported speedups relate to this study, and states that the goal here is a controlled empirical comparison of acceleration mechanisms.

\textbf{Comment 4. The case57 infeasibility pattern needs explanation.}

The revised Results text explains that the same 14 case57 dates fail across all tested modes, so this is treated as structural infeasibility of those benchmark instances rather than a failure of a specific acceleration method. The dates are: 2017-08-14, 2017-08-25, 2017-08-28, 2017-08-29, 2017-08-31, 2017-09-05, 2017-09-18, 2017-09-23, 2017-10-06, 2017-11-09, 2017-11-17, 2017-12-14, 2017-12-20, and 2017-12-25. These dates are excluded from paired speedup statistics.

\textbf{Comment 5. GNN details and negative result are missing.}

We expanded the GNN subsection. It now gives the line-graph construction, node features, critical-line threshold, inference threshold, training setup, and case300 precision/recall/F1. The paper now treats the GNN as a negative ablation: it learns a plausible classifier, but does not improve downstream MILP runtime over the simpler 1-NN margin rule.

\textbf{Comment 6. BANDIT reward, candidate set, and epsilon are missing; gain is marginal.}

We added the exact candidate set $K\in\{64,128,256\}$, the reward $R_t=-N_t^{\mathrm{added}}$, and the $\varepsilon$-greedy policy with $\varepsilon=0.1$. The Results section now states that BANDIT adds fewer cuts but gives only marginal improvement over plain LAZY, so it is reported mainly as a negative result on adaptive cut-budgeting.

\textbf{Comment 7. GRU warm starts degrade speedup and need explanation.}

We added an explanation in the Warm Starts subsection. The GRU predicts MSE-trained dispatch fractions, which do not preserve ramping and minimum up/down relationships as well as copying a solved 1-NN dispatch. On small cases, PyTorch inference overhead is also not negligible relative to total solve time. We therefore report GRU as a secondary ablation rather than as a recommended default.

\textbf{Comment 8. The main test cases are small and larger data should be considered.}

We added a limitation in the Results section. Case14 and case30 are now described as correctness and scaling sanity checks, not as practically meaningful speedup evidence. The practical claims are based mainly on case89pegase, case118, case300, and the case1354pegase stress test.

\textbf{Comment 9. Statistical significance measures are missing.}

We added bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals for representative speedup summaries and paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests against RAW. The full text reports that the main methods are significant on every benchmark case.

\textbf{Comment 10. No cross-validation; seed sensitivity is possible.}

We added this as a limitation in the Results section. The learning components use one fixed 70/15/15 split, so learning effects should be interpreted as split-specific. Seed sensitivity is listed as future work.

\textbf{Comment 11. No code or data repository.}

The scripts, benchmark configuration files, run logs, generated tables, and plotting scripts are available from the author upon request.

\textbf{Comment 12. The conclusion claim is too broad.}

We narrowed the conclusion. It now says ``among the methods considered in this work'' and refers to audited feasibility for the tested benchmark instances, not to a universal guarantee for all SCUC models.

\section*{Response to Reviewer 2}

\textbf{Point 1. Formulas (12), (15), and (21) have wrong lower-bound signs.}

We rewrote the base-case and post-contingency flow constraints in symmetric two-sided form. The lower bounds now use $-F^{\mathrm{N}}$ and $-F^{\mathrm{E}}$.

\textbf{Point 2. The z-score distance makes uniformly shifted load profiles indistinguishable.}

We changed the feature description. The 1-NN feature vector now combines the z-scored load series with level-sensitive statistics: mean load, maximum load, total load, and standard deviation. This removes the degeneracy caused by pure z-scoring.

\textbf{Point 3. Explain whether the findings reduce to common MIP experience; discuss solver behavior and lazy constraints.}

We added a solver-behavior discussion in the Results section. It explains that RAW and WARM expose the full explicit N-1 model at the root, while LAZY and PRUNE shrink the root relaxation to about 5\% of the RAW row count. The text also explains why warm starts mainly affect the primal side, whereas constraint management changes the root LP size, early gap behavior, and search burden.

We also clarified why only post-contingency constraints are made lazy. They are by far the most numerous constraints and their slack-penalty structure weakens the dual bound. Base-case limits are kept in the root LP because they are comparatively few. A preliminary experiment with lazy base-case limits did not give consistent improvement.

\textbf{Point 4. Include information about Gurobi auto-tuning.}

We added a representative \texttt{grbtune} sanity check. The tuned parameter set did not explain the order-of-magnitude speedups from constraint management: it gave about 0.93x on a representative case118 instance and 1.02x on case300.

\section*{Final Note}

The revised manuscript is shorter and more conservative. It now emphasizes that the main contribution is an empirical comparison of strong acceleration families for DC-SCUC. It also reports negative and marginal findings plainly: GNN does not improve over the 1-NN screener, GRU is not a recommended default on the tested small and medium cases, and BANDIT gives little improvement over plain LAZY.
